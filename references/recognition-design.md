# Recognition System Design

Recognition is not a nice-to-have. It is the primary mechanism by
which a contribution economy communicates what it values. Get it
wrong and you train your community to do the wrong things.

---

## The recognition hierarchy

Not all recognition is equal. In order of impact:

**1. Economic recognition**
Revenue share, equity, tokens, paid contracts, bounties. The
strongest signal that contribution is valued. Not always possible
but always the most meaningful when it is.

**2. Structural recognition**
Titles, roles, governance rights, decision-making input. Being
given a role with real responsibility signals trust and value more
than any badge.

**3. Access recognition**
Early access to products, invite-only programs, direct access to
founders or leadership, insider information. Signals that the
contributor is part of the inner circle.

**4. Public attribution**
Specific, visible credit for specific work. Release notes,
changelogs, social posts, community announcements. Must be
specific to be meaningful.

**5. Social recognition**
Community acknowledgment, peer nominations, spotlight features.
Powerful when it comes from peers rather than the organization.

**6. Symbolic recognition**
Badges, swag, certificates. Lowest impact. Can reinforce other
recognition but cannot replace it.

Most programs over-invest in tier 6 and under-invest in tiers 1-3.

---

## Design principles

**Match recognition to contribution value, not visibility**
The person who gave a keynote is visible. The person who answered
300 questions this year is valuable. Recognition systems that only
reward the visible will eventually hollow out the community.

**Be specific**
Generic recognition ("thanks for being an awesome community member")
signals that the organization didn't actually notice what the person
did. Specific recognition ("the issue you wrote in March became the
basis for our Q3 roadmap") signals that it did.

**Be timely**
Recognition delayed is recognition diminished. If someone did
something valuable six months ago and is only being recognized now,
the signal is muddled. Build mechanisms for rapid acknowledgment.

**Make it cumulative**
One-time recognition is weaker than cumulative recognition. A
system that tracks contribution over time and unlocks new
recognition levels based on sustained contribution is more
motivating than one that treats each contribution as isolated.

**Let peers participate**
Peer nominations and community-driven recognition carry more
credibility than top-down recognition in most communities. Build
peer input into the system.

---

## Common failure modes

**The popularity contest**
Recognition driven by votes or likes rewards the popular and
visible — not the most valuable. Combine peer input with
structured criteria to prevent this.

**The same 10 people**
If your recognition program always surfaces the same contributors,
it's measuring visibility, not value. Audit for this quarterly.

**The performative program**
A recognition program that was designed for the press release, not
the contributor. Contributors can tell the difference immediately.

**Recognition without change**
Recognizing contributors while the underlying system that makes
their work hard remains unchanged. Recognition must be paired with
structural improvement to be credible.
