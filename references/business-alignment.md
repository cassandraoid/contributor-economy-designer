# Aligning Community Work to Business Outcomes

The single most common reason community programs get cut is that
the people running them cannot draw a clear line from community
activity to outcomes leadership cares about. This is not a
communication problem. It is a design problem.

---

## The alignment framework

Community work aligns to business outcomes through four pathways:

**1. Pipeline**
Community members become customers, or influence customers. This
is the most direct path and the one leadership understands fastest.

Signals that indicate pipeline impact:
- Community members who later appear in CRM as leads or customers
- Community-sourced product feedback that accelerates deals
- Community champions who influence enterprise purchase decisions
- Developer adoption that drives bottom-up enterprise growth

**2. Product**
Community input improves the product, which improves retention and
expansion. Harder to attribute but often higher value than pipeline.

Signals that indicate product impact:
- Issues or feedback from community that shipped as features
- Bug reports that prevented churn-causing incidents
- Use cases discovered in community that expanded the roadmap
- Integration or extension contributions that expanded the platform

**3. Talent**
Community members become employees, or refer employees. Often
invisible in attribution systems but high value.

Signals that indicate talent impact:
- Hires sourced from or referred by community
- Contributors who become contractors or advisors
- Community reputation that improves recruiting yield

**4. Brand**
Community activity builds awareness and credibility that reduces
CAC and improves conversion. The hardest to attribute but often
the foundation for everything else.

Signals that indicate brand impact:
- Organic mentions, shares, and amplification
- Conference talks and content from community members
- Community reputation in developer surveys and rankings
- Inbound interest attributed to community visibility

---

## Making the case to leadership

Leadership needs three things to fund and support community work:

**A clear theory of value**
"Our community creates value by X, which leads to Y, which affects
Z business metric." Write this out explicitly. If you can't, the
program isn't designed for outcomes.

**Leading indicators**
Lagging indicators like revenue influenced take 12-18 months to
show up. Leadership needs to see something moving in the right
direction now. Design leading indicators that predict the lagging
ones: active contributor growth predicts pipeline, community NPS
predicts retention, contribution quality predicts product impact.

**A counterfactual**
What would happen without this program? The strongest business
case is not "we produced X" but "without us, X would not have
happened." Build attribution systems that capture this.

---

## The alignment audit

Five questions to assess current alignment:

1. Can you name five community members who became customers or
   influenced a deal in the last 12 months?
2. Can you name three pieces of community feedback that shipped
   as product improvements?
3. Can you name two hires that came from or through the community?
4. What metric does your CEO care most about? Can you draw a line
   from community activity to that metric?
5. If your program was cut tomorrow, what business outcome would
   visibly suffer within 90 days?

If you can't answer these, the program is not yet designed for
alignment. That's fixable — but it requires intentional design,
not better reporting.
