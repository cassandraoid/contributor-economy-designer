# Stakeholder Buy-In Framework

Most well-designed contribution programs never get implemented —
not because the design was wrong, but because the right people
weren't brought along at the right time. This framework helps
community builders navigate organizational buy-in before, during,
and after program design.

---

## Why buy-in fails

Three patterns account for most failed rollouts:

**Designing in isolation**
The community team designs a complete program, presents it to
leadership, and gets blocked by concerns that could have been
addressed in the design phase. By the time objections surface,
the team is attached to the design and leadership feels like
they're being asked to rubber-stamp rather than shape.

**Wrong room, wrong time**
Bringing a contribution program to a CFO before it has a clear
business case. Bringing it to Legal after the design is locked.
Bringing it to HR after you've already announced it to the
community. Sequencing matters as much as substance.

**No theory of value**
Walking into a leadership meeting without a clear answer to
"why does this matter to the business" — not to the community,
to the business. Every stakeholder will ask this. If the answer
isn't ready, the program stalls.

## Stakeholder map

Different stakeholders care about different things. Address each
on their own terms.

**Executive leadership (CEO, VP)**
Cares about: business outcomes, competitive positioning,
team efficiency, brand reputation
Speaks in: revenue, growth, risk, market position
What they need to say yes: a clear theory of value connecting
community contribution to a metric they already care about,
and a credible answer to "what does this cost us"

**Head of Product**
Cares about: roadmap input quality, user insights,
feature adoption, reducing support burden
Speaks in: user research, retention, NPS, time-to-value
What they need to say yes: evidence that community contributors
produce better product insight than other channels, and a clear
process for how community input reaches the roadmap

**Marketing / Demand Gen**
Cares about: brand awareness, content production,
community-influenced pipeline, CAC reduction
Speaks in: MQLs, content volume, share of voice, attribution
What they need to say yes: examples of community-generated
content or advocacy that influenced awareness or pipeline

**Legal**
Cares about: liability, IP ownership, compliance,
contractor classification risk
Speaks in: risk, exposure, precedent
What they need to say yes: clarity on what contributors
are and are not being asked to do, and assurance that
recognition mechanisms don't create employment classification
issues. Involve Legal early — never present a finished design.

**HR / People**
Cares about: culture consistency, fairness,
recognition equity, employee vs contractor lines
Speaks in: culture, equity, policy, process
What they need to say yes: that the program treats
contributors fairly and consistently, and that it doesn't
create a two-tier recognition culture that makes employees
feel undervalued relative to community members

**Finance**
Cares about: cost, ROI, budget predictability
Speaks in: cost per outcome, ROI, headcount equivalents
What they need to say yes: a clear budget, a way to
measure return, and a ceiling on exposure

## Sequencing buy-in

The order matters as much as the ask.

**Phase 1 — Shape with leadership before designing**
Before designing anything, have a 30-minute conversation with
your executive sponsor. Ask: what does success look like for
you in 12 months? What would make you proud of this program?
What concerns do you have before we start?

This does two things: it surfaces blockers early, and it gives
leadership co-ownership of the program before it exists. People
defend what they helped create.

**Phase 2 — Involve Product and Marketing in design**
Bring Product and Marketing into the design phase as
contributors, not reviewers. Ask Product: what community
input would be most valuable to you and how should it reach
you? Ask Marketing: what community activity would help you
most and how would you amplify it?

Design the program to answer their questions. They become
internal champions.

**Phase 3 — Brief Legal and HR before finalizing**
Before locking the design, brief Legal and HR on what you're
planning. Frame it as: "I want to make sure we're designing
this right from the start — what do I need to know?"

This prevents the most common kill scenario: a finished design
that Legal or HR blocks on principle because they weren't
involved. It also gives them ownership over the parts they
care about.

**Phase 4 — Present to Finance with a clear ask**
Present to Finance last, with a clear budget, a clear theory
of value, and leading indicators that will show momentum before
lagging metrics arrive. Finance rarely kills programs with
clear ROI frameworks — they kill programs that can't answer
basic questions about cost and return.

**Phase 5 — Announce to the community after internal alignment**
Never announce a contribution program to the community before
internal stakeholders are aligned. Community expectations once
set are very hard to walk back. Get full internal sign-off first.

## The one-page business case

Every contribution program needs a one-page internal brief that
answers five questions:

1. **What are we building?** One sentence describing the program.
2. **Why does it matter to the business?** The specific business
   outcome this program influences — in language leadership uses.
3. **What does it cost?** Time, headcount, tooling. Be specific.
4. **How will we know it's working?** Two or three leading
   indicators that will show momentum within 90 days.
5. **What's the risk of not doing it?** The cost of inaction —
   contributor attrition, competitive disadvantage, team
   inefficiency.

If you cannot answer all five clearly, the program is not
ready to present. That is not a failure — it means the design
work is not done yet.

## Handling common objections

**"We don't have budget for this."**
Most contribution programs don't require significant budget —
they require design and consistency. Reframe around what you're
asking for: time and process change, not spend. If there is a
cost, tie it to a specific outcome: "X investment in contributor
recognition is projected to reduce community support burden by Y."

**"How do we know this will work?"**
Propose a time-boxed pilot with clear success criteria. 90 days,
defined metrics, defined scope. Lower the commitment required
to get started. A pilot is much easier to say yes to than a
permanent program.

**"This feels like a lot of overhead."**
Show the cost of the current system — specifically the cost of
contributor attrition, invisible labor going untracked, and
community work that isn't influencing the business. The overhead
of a designed system is almost always lower than the hidden cost
of an undesigned one.

**"Legal needs to review this."**
"Absolutely — I'd love to get them involved early so we can
design this right from the start. Can you make that introduction?"
