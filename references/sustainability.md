# Contributor Sustainability

A contribution economy that burns out its best contributors in
12 months is not a success. Sustainability is not a soft concern —
it is the primary measure of whether a contribution system is
actually working.

---

## The burnout pattern

Contributor burnout follows a predictable pattern. Recognizing it
early is the most important intervention available.

**Stage 1 — Early warning (3-6 months before departure)**
- Contribution frequency remains high but quality starts to decline
- Response time to community questions increases
- Tone in communications becomes more transactional
- Stops going beyond the minimum
- Disengages from non-task community interactions

**Stage 2 — Active burnout (1-3 months before departure)**
- Contribution frequency starts to decline
- Misses commitments or deadlines they would previously have kept
- Shorter, more closed-ended responses
- Direct expressions of frustration in public or private channels
- Stops participating in community spaces beyond required tasks

**Stage 3 — Exit preparation**
- Contribution frequency drops sharply
- Goes quiet without explanation
- Stops responding to direct messages
- May explicitly express intent to reduce involvement

**Stage 4 — Departure**
- Full disengagement
- Often leaves without announcement
- Takes institutional knowledge with them

Most programs intervene at Stage 3 or 4. Effective programs
intervene at Stage 1.

---

## Structural causes of burnout

Individual burnout is almost always a system design problem, not
a personal failure.

**Contribution concentration**
When too few people carry too much load, burnout is inevitable.
The solution is system design — creating pathways for load
distribution before the core group hits their limit.

**Invisible labor accumulation**
Contributors who do a lot of invisible labor carry a hidden
load that doesn't show up in any metric. They often don't
realize how much they're doing until they stop.

**Recognition debt**
When contribution consistently exceeds recognition, contributors
develop recognition debt — a felt sense that they are giving more
than they are getting back. This is the most common precursor
to departure.

**Boundary erosion**
Communities that normalize always-on availability, rapid response
expectations, and unlimited scope creep erode contributor
boundaries over time. This is a culture problem that requires
explicit design to counteract.

**Lack of progression**
Contributors who have been doing the same work for 2+ years with
no change in status, responsibility, or reward are at elevated
departure risk. Contribution economies need progression systems.

---

## Sustainability design interventions

**Design for load distribution from the start**
Don't wait for concentration to become a crisis. Build explicit
mechanisms for distributing contribution load: co-maintainer
programs, rotating responsibilities, contribution area ownership
that distributes rather than concentrates.

**Build pacing into the culture**
Explicitly model sustainable contribution. Celebrate consistency
over intensity. Acknowledge when contributors take breaks. Make
it normal to say "I need to step back for a month."

**Create progression pathways**
Every contribution level should have a clear path to the next
one. Contributors should know what sustained engagement earns
them — not just in recognition, but in responsibility, access,
and economic participation.

**Address recognition debt proactively**
Audit for contributors whose recognition is significantly below
their contribution value. Address it before they bring it up —
or before they leave without bringing it up.

**Make it safe to reduce contribution**
The most sustainable communities make it easy to step back
temporarily without feeling like departure. Build explicit
mechanisms for contribution sabbaticals, reduced involvement
periods, and re-engagement pathways.
