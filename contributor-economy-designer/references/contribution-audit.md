# Contribution Audit Framework

Use this when someone needs to assess the current state of their
contribution system before designing improvements.

---

## The audit starts with visibility

Before evaluating whether a contribution system is working, you need
to map what's actually being contributed. Most audits skip this step
and go straight to metrics — which means they only measure what was
already visible.

### Step 1: Map the full contribution surface

Work through every place value is being created in the community:

**Technical contributions**
- Code (PRs, bug fixes, features)
- Documentation (writing, editing, translating)
- Testing and QA
- Infrastructure and tooling
- Security research and disclosure

**Community contributions**
- Question answering (forums, Discord, Slack, Stack Overflow)
- New member onboarding and welcoming
- Event organizing and facilitation
- Content creation (tutorials, videos, blog posts)
- Conference talks and representation

**Strategic contributions**
- Issue writing that shapes the roadmap
- Feedback that changes product direction
- Competitive intelligence
- Partnership and integration building
- Recruiting and referring other contributors

**Invisible contributions**
- Conflict mediation and community moderation
- Emotional support to struggling contributors
- Institutional memory and knowledge transfer
- Mentorship through DMs
- Showing up consistently over years without recognition

### Step 2: Score current visibility

For each contribution type, rate current visibility:
- **Visible** — tracked, attributed, and publicly acknowledged
- **Partial** — sometimes tracked but inconsistently
- **Invisible** — happens regularly but receives no recognition

The gap between what's happening and what's visible is where the
system is failing.

### Step 3: Identify concentration

Map who is doing what. Look for:
- Any function where one person does 60%+ of the work
- Any function where the same small group appears repeatedly
- Any function with no backup — single points of failure
- Any demographic pattern in who does invisible vs visible work

### Step 4: Assess recognition proportionality

Compare the most recognized contributors against the most
valuable contributors. These are often different people. The gap
between them is a retention risk.

### Step 5: Identify the highest-leverage gaps

After the audit, prioritize three gaps:
1. The contribution type with highest value and lowest visibility
2. The person or group most at risk of burnout or departure
3. The metric leadership cares about that the current system
   can't demonstrate

These three gaps define the design brief.

---

## Audit questions to ask

For existing programs:
- What contributions does your current program formally recognize?
- Who are your most recognized contributors? What did they do?
- Who are your most active contributors? Are they the same people?
- When did you last lose a contributor you didn't want to lose?
  What happened?
- What work is happening in your community that nobody is tracking?
- Can a contributor see their own impact in your system?

For new programs:
- What does this community need people to do that you can't hire for?
- What would make someone feel that contributing here was worth it?
- What are the three things leadership will ask you to prove about
  this program in 12 months?
